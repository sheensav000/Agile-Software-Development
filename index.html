Agile

What is Agile Software Development?

Agile is a methodology commonly used within software development that prioritizes customer feedback, faster delivery times, setting priorities, and reducing waste.

Why is Agile important?

Software developers found that they were frustrated by spending so much time working on their code and fixing every bug while completing a lot of documentation. They felt like this was a waste of time. With Agile, work is done in short iterations. The features and products that come out are not perfect. However, this brings more feedback directly from customers and developers can quickly make changes and release new features.

These iterations are also called the Agile Software Development Life Cycle and are repeated in sprints. These sprints last from 2-4 weeks.

What does this look like?

The most popular framework within Agile is Scrum. Scrum focuses on collaboration within teams to complete the product backlog.

First, the team leader will meet with shareholders to look at all of the possible projects to be completed and the priority of those projects are decided.

Then, the team leader meets with their Scrum team of software developers to split projects into small tasks.

Individually, the software developers decide what they can complete in a sprint and are in charge of those tasks. Teams meet for daily standups that are only 15 minutes long to hold each other accountable.

Lastly, at the end of each iteration, teams meet to take a retrospective look at the sprint. They may receive feedback that the current project is not something customers want anymore and they will pivot to a different project and release new features that customers do want.

Agile is highly adaptable to change.

Key terms

Agile Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC): Each iteration consists of six steps. Requirements, Design, Development, Testing, Deployment, and Review. Learn more about what these mean by hovering over the relative box.

Sprint: Sprints are a set period of time, typically 2-4 weeks for each iteration of the Software Development Life Cycle.

Kanban: Kanban allows to visually see tasks on Kanban cards to see the tasks to be completed and their progress. Within Agile, these cards are typically used on a Kanban board.

Scrum: Scrum is a popular framework within Agile that promotes working in short cycles, delivering often, meeting daily, and managing a backlog.

Scrum Master: The Scrum Master facilitates the team by managing the product backlog (see below) and encouraging the use of Agile Scrum principles.

Product Backlog: The product backlog is the list, set by priority, of the tasks to be completed in a sprint. Backlogs are organized by the Scrum Master, with input from stakeholders.

Is Agile only used for software development?

Nope! There are several ways that Agile can be implemented, not only in business, but at home and in personal life as well.

Consider how this can be used in a classroom setting. If you're working on a school project, getting continuous feedback can really improve your final submission.

If you're working on a large project, you write the thesis first, get feedback on that part, then quickly write and revise your body paragraphs. Finally, the conclusion is written once you have a polished essay after implementing comments from peers.

Agile SDLC

Learn more about each step of the Agile process below.

Requirements

Requirements for the project and task are determined. Stakeholders conduct overall assessment and owners assess risks.

Design

Meet as a team and introduces requirements. Discuss the order for introducing features. What are the tools needed? What frameworks will be used?

Development

Develop the product. Scrum and Kanban methods are used here. Delivered in stages in separate sprints.

Testing

Does it work properly? Fix bugs immediately. Collect feedback.

Deployment

Feature is fully developed and available to customers. Maintenance happens here with ongoing support.

Review

Present to the owner and review results compared to requirements. Then start over at Step 1 for the next sprint.

How was Agile used to complete this project?

I had two weeks to complete this project. These two weeks could be considered one sprint. Then, I release the product by submitting it and getting feedback from peers, TAs, and instructors. There are a couple ways to approach the next sprint. I could take the feedback to improve this project. However, doing more work for this project may not be in the best interest for my grade or learning, and I would move on to complete another project. A key element of Agile is being adaptable to change and quickly producing high-quality work.

Additionally, the most important parts of the project should be prioritized first with smaller, less important features done last. With this project, I first completed Level 1 Research to get an overview of the topic. Then, I found a template for the website and formatted the main page how I wanted it so I knew what direction I wanted to go with my Level 2 Research. The next task was completing Level 2 Research. I added the graphic to the page and matched the squares to the graphic with titles and color. The content of the page was next. Then, smaller features like font size, styling, and paragraph breaks came last.

Using Agile to create this website helped me create a high quality product in a short amount of time by focusing on the most important parts first.
